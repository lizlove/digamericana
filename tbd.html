About us

Americana Music Productions (AMP) was born of the reality that as rich as the known landscape of American music is, there are far more riches that remain unknown, unsung, and undiscovered. This threat to our cultural legacy is real, cutting across all types of materials and sparing no musical tradition.

Founded in 2013, AMP leverages relationships with some of the world's top archives, record companies, museums, radio stations, libraries, filmmakers, researchers, photographers, lawyers, writers, and musicians
to build collaborative teams that address complex problems (like preservation, licensing, and resources) project-by-project, enabling access in the service of preserving America's vast musical heritage.


Recent Work

Recent Work

AMP approaches each project with love and respect for the creative act, the artist, and the community from which it emanates. While much of our focus is in the past, we see American musical traditions as a living organism transcending time and place. Our particular work is to provide the raw materials to nourish and inspire the creative spirit, musical or otherwise. We believe these sensitivities not only create better work, but also foster greater understanding of the music we celebrate.

We're currently at work on...
A limited edition LP of Mable Hillery and Johnny Shines at the 1975 Miami Blues Festival
Researching the work of feminist music historian Rosetta Reitz and her flagship Rosetta Records
And helping blues and R&B great Dennis Binder with his archive and autobiographical manuscript about the music scene and integration in Lawton, OK and the Southwest

In the past we've...
Introduced the 1969 Ann Arbor Blues Festival to attendees of the Barnard-Columbia Blues Symposium
Written an piece on the "Southern Journey" in Sounding Out! to celebrate Alan Lomax's centennial
Interviewed the late New Orleans drummer Smokey Johnson about his life, Fats Domino, and early Motown
Researched and licensed photos and film footage for the National Blues Museum in St. Louis
Produced an 6-hour Willie Dixon Centennial radio program on WKCR-FM in NYC
